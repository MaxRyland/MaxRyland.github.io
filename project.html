Economics X Health:
How can economic prosperity improve health?

by Max Ryland

There is a general consensus that economic prosperity is thought to improve the health of a country. Indeed, citizens of richer countries typically suffer less from diseases and illnesses, and live longer lives.


The chart on the right shows the relationship between economic prosperity and longevity of a population. We see that GDP per capita is positively correlated with life expectancy at birth among OECD countries. For every 10.000 dollar increase in GDP per capita life expectancy at birth increases by 0.7 years.


Furthermore, we see from the below chart that the top 3 healthiest OECD countries, according to the Bloomberg Health Index, are highly developed economies with high GDP/capita levels. Whilst the 3 least healthiest countries are poorer, less developed nations.





While economic prosperity undeniably leads to longer and healthier lives, the aim of this project is to go beyond this main wisdom and examine how exactly does economic prosperity cause good health.


The first channel of interest that we will explore is how economic prosperity may affect the supply side of health. Indeed, the fruits of economic growth would allow governments to increase spending on public health services.


We can observe this mechanism from the below chart. As world GDP per capita has increased over the course of the 21st century so too has health expenditure per capita. In fact, both measures seem to increase in tandem, as GDP and Health spending have roughly doubled since 2000.







Health spending increases are the result of economic growth. Additional health spending allows national health services to invest in human and physical capital which improves the country’s supply of health and, by definition, the quality of overall health.


We consider this theory by studying South Korea’s supply of healthcare. South Korea has undergone incredible economic growth in the last half century, averaging a 6.34% annual GDP growth rate since 1990, during which the country has transitioned from a developing to a developed country. As expected, South Korea’s healthcare provision has significantly improved alongside its economic prosperity.





We see vast improvements to healthcare resources in South Korea. The number of hospital beds available have more than tripled and CT scanners and MRI units, which help physicians diagnose a range of conditions by producing images of internal organs and structures of the body, have increased twofold and sixfold, respectively. The numbers of nurses and doctors have also doubled in the last 20 years.


In sum, economic prosperity improves health through increased spending on healthcare.







The second channel through which economic growth contributes to better health is through better nutrition. The theory goes that economic prosperity increases incomes which in turn increases the consumption of calories and micronutrients. Well-nourished individuals are in turn in a better position to resist bacterial diseases and have better prospects to recover from illnesses. This is particularly beneficial for better health outcomes in developing countries.


As malnutrition is particularly widespread throughout the African continent, we will test this theory by studying the continent’s fastest growing economies; Ethiopia, Kenya, Rwanda, Nigeria, Ghana. Chart 5 shows these countries have had tremendous growth over the last two decades.







Stunting, or growth retardation, is the result of long-term nutritional deprivation and has severe consequences on long-term health, putting the individual at greater risk of illness and death.


Also, small women are at greater risk of delivering an infant with low birth weight, contributing to the intergenerational cycle of malnutrition, as infants of low birth weight tend be smaller as adults.


There is general trend of decreasing malnutrition among the fast growing economies of Africa, supporting the previously outlined theory. The prevalence of stunting of under 5 year olds has significantly diminished in the time frame of these country's economic prosperity.









While other factors also affect health, it is reasonable to suggest that economic prosperity positively impacts food provision and thus population health in developing countries.


To conclusion, through the means of improved governmental health provision and better nutrition, we have shown how economic prosperity delivers better health outcomes. Life expectancy in South Korea and the selected African countries has considerably improved during the last two decades of economic prosperity.
