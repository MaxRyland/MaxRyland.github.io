Economics X Health:
How can economic prosperity improve health?

by Max Ryland

Home Portfolio About me



There is a general consensus that economic prosperity is thought to improve the health of a country. Indeed, citizens of richer countries typically suffer less from diseases and illnesses, and live longer lives.


The chart on the right shows the relationship between economic prosperity and longevity of a population. We see that GDP per capita is positively correlated with life expectancy at birth among OECD countries. For every 10.000 dollar increase in GDP per capita life expectancy at birth increases by 0.7 years.


Furthermore, we see from the below chart that the top 3 healthiest countries in the world, according to the Bloomberg Health Index, are highly developed economies with high GDP/capita levels. Whilst the 3 least healthiest countries are poorer, less developed nations.





While economic prosperity undeniably leads to longer and healthier lives, the aim of this project is to go beyond this main wisdom and examine how exactly does economic prosperity cause good health.


The first channel of interest that we will explore is how economic prosperity may affect the supply side of health. Indeed, the fruits of economic growth would allow governments to increase spending on public health services.


We can observe this mechanism from the below chart. As world GDP per capita has increased over the course of the 21st century so too has health expenditure per capita. In fact, both measures seem to increase in tandem, as GDP and Health spending have roughly doubled since 2000.







Health spending increases are the result of economic growth. Additional health spending allows national health services to invest in human and physsical capital which improves the country’s supply of health and, by definition, the quality of overall health.


We consider this theory by studying South Korea’s supply of healthcare. South Korea has undergone incredible economic growth in the last half century, averaging a 6.34% annual GDP growth rate since 1990, during which the country has transitioned from a developing to a developed country. As expected, South Korea’s healthcare provision has significantly improved alongside its economic prosperity.





We see vast improvements to healthcare resources in South Korea. The number of hospital beds available have more than tripled and CT scanners and MRI units, which help physicians diagnose a range of conditions by producing images of internal organs and structures of the body, have increased twofold and sixfold, respectively. The numbers of nurses and doctors have also doubled in the last 20 years.


In sum, economic prosperity improves health through increased spending on healthcare.
